Mobile phone noise figure

Before your phone (or mobile device) is anything else, it is a radio. Perhaps the most important parameter of a radio recevier is its [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noise_figure noise figure]. A low noise figure means less noise, and (roughly speaking) more bandwdith.
Below we show how the noise figure of various subsystems (blocks) can be combined to form an overall noise figure, and what that means in practice. A simple problem, but one considered routinely by the [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio-frequency_engineering RF] engineers that designed your phone.
The figure shows a simplified [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RF_front_end RF front end] of a mobile phone, comprising several blocks:
  • DUP: [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duplexer duplexer]
  • LNA: [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-noise_amplifier low-noise amplifier]
  • ETC: Everthing else.
Each block has a gain and a noise figure. The question is, given the specifications for each block, what is the overall noise figure for the receiver? The code below calls a function to repeatedly apply [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friis_formulas_for_noise, Friis formula] for two blocks. The function itself appears at the end.
The output array is the cumulative noise figure looking into each block— so the total noise figure for the system (often called the "input referred" noise figure) is about 3.4 dB. We can learn quite a lot about RF design just by playing with the values above. Although very simple, the following observations dominate the RF design of mobile phones.
  • The total NF is very sensitive to loss before the LNA. Try changing the duplexer gain and noise figure to -2 dB and 2 dB rspectively.
  • The LNA is a critical block. Try chainging its gain and noise figure.
  • The total noise figure is far less sensitive to the noise figure of blocks that follow the LNA. Try changing the noise figure of the ETC block.
